## Abstract

After thirty-one papers, the reader should carry away four things: where Buddha-recollection came from, how it evolved, how to practice it, and what that practice actually does internally. This paper is not an epilogue, not a map, not a collection of seal-lines. It is a clarification—stripping away the doctrinal layers of thirty-one papers to expose the internal structure of *niàn fó* (念佛).

Paper One (*What Anussati Actually Means*) established that *anussati* (recollection) is not a variant of *sati* (mindfulness), but a distinct *caitta* (mental factor) with its own operational structure—fourfold rhythm: establish the object, maintain the link, arise of confidence/joy, loosen attachment. The Nine Epithets (*itipi so*) are not a list of tasks; they are a template with nine entrances, each leading to the same operation. The Six Recollections are not a list; they are a system, with Buddha-recollection at the center because the other five all depend on the Buddha's qualities as background object. These three things (fourfold rhythm, nine entrances, one system) constitute the original form of recollection, operating since the Pāli Āgamas.

From Pāli to Mahāyāna, the object of recollection expanded layer by layer—from one Buddha to the Buddhas of ten directions, from recollecting qualities to seeing the Buddha face-to-face, calling the name, to Samantabhadra's ten great vows extending the object to the entire Dharma-realm. The operation did not change. What changed is what can be seen at each layer. The question in Chinese tradition was never "What is recollection?" but "How many layers can recollection have?"

The core task of this paper is clarification: What is the internal operation of recollection? It is not transactional—one chant does not equal merit, one visualization does not equal blessing. The internal operation of recollection is one thing only: changing the structure of the mind. Any of the nine entrances of the Nine Epithets is sufficient to begin this change. The eight characters of "Gathering the six sense roots, maintaining pure recollection continuously" (T0945) constitute the simplest expression of this change. Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows constitute its complete framework.

After walking through thirty-one papers, the essence of *niàn* (念, recollection) has not changed. *Niàn* is *niàn*. From the Nine Epithets to the Dharma-realm, all doctrine, all sūtras, all patriarchs served only to let this simple fact appear.

**Keywords:** Buddha-recollection; internal mechanics; Nine Epithets; *anussati*; fourfold rhythm; gathering the six sense roots; pure recollection continuously; Samantabhadra Ten Great Vows; Dharma-realm recollection; internal operation of recollection; from Pāli to Mahāyāna; transactional practice critique

## I. The Origin of Buddha-Recollection—From *Anussati* to *Niàn Fó*

Paper One already made clear how it begins.

*Anussati* is not mindfulness. It is not "a religious version of attention." It is a distinct *caitta* (mental factor), with its own operational structure, its own object domain, its own effect mechanism.

Its operational structure has four layers.

**Layer One: Establishing the object.** The first step of recollection is not recollection itself; it is selecting the object. This object is not random—it must be an *ajjhāyatana* (曾習境), an object previously encountered and capable of being clearly presented in the mind. For recollection, this object is typically the Buddha's qualities. The Buddha's qualities are not singular—they are a set of nine epithets (*itipi so*). Each epithet is an independent entrance, not a task on a list. Which one to choose depends on the mind's current state.

**Layer Two: Maintaining the link.** After selecting the object, the next step is not stopping at "seeing it," but maintaining the link with it. This maintenance is not a tautened string—it requires sustained but loosened intensity. Pāli texts describe this state with the term *ajjhāyatana* (持續的所緣, persistent object). The Chinese tradition translates this as "念" (niàn), which does not mean "attention" but "remembering"—remembering the object, holding the object, letting the object persist in the mind.

**Layer Three: The arising of confidence/joy.** When the object is stably maintained for a period, an effect automatically arises: confidence or joy. This is not the result of effort; it is the natural product of the operation. Like watching a lamp you have been staring at steadily—the lamp does not get brighter, but you see light flowing in your eyes. The joy of recollection is not religious emotion; it is the natural state that arises when the link between mind and object is established.

**Layer Four: Loosening attachment.** After confidence arises, the next step is loosening—loosening the attachment to "I am recollecting." Not abandoning recollection, but loosening the intensity of "the recollector I." This step existed in the earliest *anussati*, merely unmarked. In later Mahāyāna, it becomes increasingly explicit as the internal goal of recollection.

These four steps are not four stages—they occur simultaneously in every recollection, analyzable separately but operationally unified.

The nine entrances of the Nine Epithets each lead through these four steps. The nine epithets are:

1. *Araham*—Worthy One: The Buddha who has eliminated all defilements and deserves all offerings
2. *Svākkhāto*—Well-Taught: The Dharma taught correctly and without error
3. *Samādito*—Properly Practiced: The path taught correctly
4. *Dakkhiyeyya*—Worthy of Offerings: The supreme field of merit in the world
5. *Anuttaro*—Unequalled: No awakening surpasses the Buddha's
6. *Purisadamma-sārathi*—Teacher of Beings to be Tamed: The Buddha who can tame all beings
7. *Satthā deva-manussānaṃ*—Teacher of Deva and Human: The Buddha who is the teacher of devas and humans
8. *Buddha*—Awakened One: The Buddha who has fully awakened to all things
9. *Bhagavā*—Blessed One: The Buddha possessing both merit and wisdom

Any of these nine epithets can independently serve as the object of recollection. Selecting *Araham* or selecting *Buddha* feels different at the beginning; the operational process differs; the joy that arises differs—but the four steps remain identical. The nine entrances are given by the Buddha himself. He does not need to say "you must choose this one," because the four steps operate automatically through any entrance.

The Six Recollections are not a list. They are six interlocking operational systems: recollection of the Buddha, Dharma, Saṅgha, devas, generosity, and morality. Buddha-recollection stands at the center not because the Buddha is "greater" but because the other five all depend on the Buddha's qualities as background object. Buddha-recollection is the hub; the other five are the six rings circling it. The first ring is Buddha-recollection—not an option among six, but the entrance to the system.

From this entrance, you are already inside recollection.

## II. From Pāli to Mahāyāna—The Layered Unfolding of the Object

The object of recollection is not fixed. It expands from one Buddha to the Buddhas of ten directions, from the Buddha's qualities to the Buddha's Pure Lands, to the Buddhas of the entire Dharma-realm. This is not recollection changing; this is recollection unfolding. Each layer retains the previous. No layer is discarded.

**The Pāli recollection** (P01–P04) is clear. Recollection is *anussati*, with an explicit operational structure. The object is the Buddha's nine epithets; the operation has four layers; the effect is the arising of confidence and the loosening of attachment. This is recollection in its most primordial and clearest form—untouched by later interpretive overlays.

**The earliest Chinese reception** (P05) is narrativized. The *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* (T0784) transforms the Buddha's qualities into forty-two instructional verses. Recollection is no longer "recollecting the Buddha's qualities" but "recollecting the Buddha's teachings." This first translation shifts the meaning of "niàn fó" in Chinese tradition from primarily "recollecting the Buddha's qualities" to "recollecting the Buddha's teachings"—a shift with enduring consequences.

**The early Mahāyāna leap** (P06–P08) is the first major expansion. The *Pratyutpanna Sūtra* (T0418) introduces **face-to-face Buddha sighting**—recollection is no longer only an internal operation but the experience of seeing the Buddha face-to-face in samādhi. The arising of Pure Land concepts is the second expansion—the object of recollection is no longer only Śākyamuni Buddha but all Buddhas of ten directions. The shift from recollecting qualities to calling the name is the third expansion—the operational form shifts from mental recollection to vocal invocation.

These three expansions do not replace each other. Face-to-face sighting does not replace recollecting qualities; calling the name does not replace face-to-face sighting. They coexist, each operating in different contexts. A practitioner could recollect qualities during morning sūtra reading, practice face-to-face sighting during meditation, and call the name while walking—all on the same day. This is not "three different methods"; it is one operation unfolding naturally across different contexts.

**The Three Pure Land Sūtras' layered architecture** (P09–P11) is recollection systematized. The *Saṃghāṭī Sūtra*'s Forty-Eight Vows establish recollection's foundation on the Buddha's vows—recollection is effective not merely because the practitioner's operation is correct, but because the Buddha has already prepared a receiving mechanism. The *Contemplation Sūtra*'s sixteen contemplations form the upper layer—from the simplest name-calling to the most complex reality-contemplation, sixteen layers simultaneously open, with entrances for all capacities. The *Amida Sūtra* is the minimal architecture—only one operation: holding the name. Three sūtras, three layers, one operation at different depths.

**The Śūraṅgama Sūtra as the Practice Pivot** (P12) marks the deepest structural expansion. T0945's "Gathering the six sense roots, maintaining pure recollection continuously, attaining samādhi—this is supreme" is not the most difficult item among the eight Mahāyāna sūtras—it is the **double-axis deepening** of recollection from the Pure Land sūtras to Mahāyāna's broader framework.

P12 gives the most critical operational classification. The late-Ming commentator Jiaoguang Zhenjian (交光真鑒) in his *Śūraṅgama Śāstra Commentary* (X0275) divides the verb "recollection" into two types: **distinguishing-awareness recollection** (分別識心所念) —recollection as the sixth consciousness's activity, treating the Buddha as an object of conceptual analysis; **six-roots-integrated recollection** (攝根念) —six roots simultaneously pacified, equal in status, integrated into one Buddha-recollection. These are not two depths of the same action; they are two different types of action. In distinguishing-awareness recollection, the structure is "the recollecting consciousness vs. the recollecting object." In six-roots-integrated recollection, the structure is six roots simultaneously pacified without discursive thought, simultaneously illuminating the three bodies and four realms of the Buddha.

"Gathering the six sense roots"—Jiaoguang explains "gathering" (攝) as "令六根不動" (causing the six roots to remain still)—six roots equal and pacified, without discursive agitation. Not closing the six roots (closing eyes, silence, holding breath), but six roots still functioning (seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, touching, thinking) simultaneously without discursive response. This is sensory integration, not sensory suppression. The "都" (all/universal) of "都攝" distinguishes it from "each root's separate entry" (六根圓通)—in the latter, one specific root serves as the gate; in "都攝," all six roots integrate simultaneously into one Buddha-recollection.

"Pure recollection continuously"—Jiaoguang explains "pure" (淨) as "圓照三身四土" (simultaneously illuminating the Buddha's three bodies and four realms). One recollection, simultaneously illuminating the Buddha's dharmakāya, saṃbhogakāya, nirmāṇakāya, and all four realms of abiding. "Continuously" means this illumination is not a one-time attainment but an uninterrupted flow. But Jiaoguang honestly acknowledges: "If 'pure' is defined against 'defiled,' then pure recollection remains 'recollection'" (淨對染言之，則淨念終仍成有念)—in this binary framework, pure recollection is still "having recollection," not ultimate "no-recollection." This means maintaining pure recollection is not demanding the practitioner reach a state of pure non-recollection; it is maintaining the continuity between pure recollections within the phenomenological layer of "recollection." If one recollection is pure and the next is defiled, the repair happens immediately in the next recollection—not as a new beginning, but as the self-repairing structure of the sequence.

P12's argument is clear: T0945 is the first structural deepening of recollection from the Pure Land sūtras into Mahāyāna's broader framework. The spatial axis expands from "the mind" to "the six roots" (from single organ to full-integration model); the temporal axis extends from "not being disturbed at one moment" to "pure recollection continuously" (from momentary achievement to cross-temporal continuity). This is not a difficulty increase; it is an operational clarification. Mañjuśrī's recollection sits at position 24 among the twenty-five gates of perfect penetration (七大·根大), slightly below Avalokiteśvara at 25; but Jiaoguang's "position ≠ ranking" reading— "for those who cannot cultivate self-nirvāṇa, there is no better alternative than recollection for rebirth in the Pure Land"—establishes that position and ranking are two different axes: ontologically, all twenty-five gates are equal; operationally, the ear-root gate is first for Śākyans' self-power awakening; recollection is the first alternative "for those who cannot cultivate self-nirvāṇa."

**The Mahāyāna Eight Sūtras** (P13–P19) unfold this double-axis deepening in seven different directions. Each sūtra approaches recollection from a different angle: the Lotus Sūtra's name-invocation as universal gate ritual, the Perfect Enlightenment Sūtra's twenty-five pure samādhis for the post-nirvāṇa era, the Brahmajāla Sūtra's precept as the framework of recollection, the Golden Light Sūtra's confession as transformation through recollection, the Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra's "recollection is mind-recollection" as tathāgatagarbha realization, the Ghana-vyūha Sūtra's recollection as ālaya transformation, and the Mañjuśrī Prajñā Sūtra's one-practice samādhi as cross-sectarian witness. Seven sūtras, seven angles, one recollection—all unfolding within the structural framework established by "gathering the six roots, maintaining pure recollection."

**The Pure Land and Chan dual streams** (P20–P24) represent recollection's most mature expression in Chinese tradition. The Pure Land line from Tañluan to Dàochuò to Shàndào fully grounds recollection in the Buddha's vows—its effectiveness depends not on the practitioner's merit but on the Buddha's prepared mechanism. The Chan line from Huìnéng to Dàoxìn to Zōngmì internalizes recollection completely—recollection is mind-recollection, mind-recollection is recollection. These streams appear opposite in direction, but their operational core is identical: both change the mind's structure, only differently—one outward toward reliance, one inward toward integration. Yongmíng and Ouyì's synthesis did not merge two lines; it confirmed both lines were always valid.

**Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows of Dharma-realm recollection** (P25–P29) represent recollection's ultimate expansion. Samantabhadra's ten vows extend recollection's object from one Buddha, to ten-direction Buddhas, to the entire Dharma-realm—every being, every event, every moment. Recollection is no longer "recollection of the Buddha"; it is "recollection of the Dharma-realm." This expansion cancels no previous stage—the Nine Epithets remain valid, face-to-face sighting remains valid, name-calling remains valid. Dharma-realm recollection is recollection's widest version, not a replacement.

The trajectory of evolution ends here. From Pāli to Mahāyāna, from one Buddha to the Buddhas of the Dharma-realm, from recollecting qualities to face-to-face sighting to name-calling to Dharma-realm recollection—each step is additive, not substitutive. The core operation remains unchanged at every stage: four layers, nine entrances, one system.

## III. Recollection as Feasible Practice

After thirty-one papers of sūtras, patriarchs, and doctrine, the reader might ask: Can recollection actually be practiced?

The answer is: Yes. From the first paper, it has always been practicable.

Recollection's feasibility was established in the earliest Pāli texts. *Anussati* is not a theoretical concept; it is an operational command. When the Buddha said "recollection," he was not saying "the Buddha is great"; he was saying "do this." This operation has an object (any of the Nine Epithets), steps (the fourfold rhythm), and effects (the arising of confidence, the loosening of attachment).

From Pāli to Mahāyāna, recollection's operation has never become "harder." Rather, it has become "easier"—because more entrances have been opened.

Nine entrances from the Nine Epithets. Three layered thresholds from the Three Pure Land Sūtras. Eight angles from the Mahāyāna Eight Sūtras. Ten vow-entrances from Samantabhadra. Each entrance is sufficient to begin a complete recollection operation. Each entrance can carry you through all four layers of the fourfold rhythm.

But feasible does not mean simple. Recollection's feasibility lies in this: anyone, at any time, in any place, can begin. It requires no special equipment, no special environment, no special teacher. It requires only: an object, a duration, sustained attention.

This sounds too simple. Too simple to be trusted.

Doubt is normal, because recollection's internal operation is not what it looks like from the outside.

The internal operation of recollection has only one core: changing the structure of the mind.

Not accumulating merit. Not reducing defilement. Not accumulating blessing. Not exchanging for future rebirth. These are all transactional misreadings—treating recollection as a system of merit currency. Recollection is not a currency system. It is a structural operation.

Your mind is a system. This system has a structure: six roots' perception → six consciousnesses' discrimination → manas' attachment → manovijñāna's processing. This structure operates automatically in everyday states, requiring nothing from you. But you can insert an operation into it: recollection.

Recollection inserts at the point between six roots and six consciousnesses. When you recollect, the six roots no longer flow outward to six objects; they flow toward the object of recollection. This shift happens automatically—you do not need to "effortfully look away"; you only need to sustain attention on the recollection object. When the six roots' flow changes, the six consciousnesses' object changes, manas' attachment object changes, manovijñāna's content changes. The entire structure is changed.

This is not metaphor. This is the Yogācāra mechanism. Paper Eighteen (密嚴經) explained *ālaya-parāvṛtti* (阿賴耶轉依, transformation of the storehouse consciousness). Paper Thirty explained the manas-transformation mechanism.

But mechanism is not instruction. Knowing "recollection changes mind structure" does not equal knowing "how to make recollection change mind structure."

So we need to go further—into the internal steps of recollection.

## IV. The Internal Steps of Recollection

Recollection's internal operation has four layers. These four layers occur simultaneously in every recollection, analyzable separately but operationally unified. They are not four stages—they are four simultaneous aspects.

**Layer One: Establishing the object.** The first step of recollection is not vocal chanting, not mental repetition, not breath-numbering. It is establishing the object.

The object is what the mind focuses on. The object of recollection is not the four characters "Amitābha Buddha"—those are only sound or text. The object is the Buddha's qualities that the four characters bring into the mind. Each epithet of the Nine Epithets is an independent object. Selecting *Araham* (Worthy One), what arises in your mind is "the Buddha who has eliminated all defilements, deserving all offerings." Selecting *Bhagavā* (Blessed One), what arises is "the Buddha possessing both merit and wisdom." Different objects produce different psychological states during operation—but the fourfold rhythm remains identical.

There are several ways to establish the object: closing your eyes and vividly presenting the Buddha's form in your mind; looking at a Buddha image; focusing only on the sound of the name; focusing only on the qualities the name brings. All four ways can establish the object—the key is not which you choose, but whether the object exists clearly in your mind. If the object is vague, recollection is merely repeated sound or text, not *anussati*.

But "establishing the object" has a hidden layer—how you establish the object determines whether your recollection is "distinguishing-awareness recollection" or "six-roots-integrated recollection." Jiaoguang's verb classification makes this decisive: similarly calling "recollection," distinguishing-awareness recollection treats the Buddha as an object of conceptual analysis by the sixth consciousness; six-roots-integrated recollection is six roots simultaneously pacified and equal, integrated into one Buddha-recollection. These are not two depths of the same action; they are two different types of action. The first structure is "the recollecting consciousness vs. the recollecting object." The second structure is six roots simultaneously pacified without discursive thought, simultaneously illuminating the three bodies and four realms. How you establish the object determines which type of action you are using. Which epithet you select (*Araham* or *Bhagavā*) does not determine the action type; *how* you select it does. If, after selecting an epithet, you analyze it conceptually—this is distinguishing-awareness recollection. If, after selecting an epithet, you let it naturally present in your mind and integrate your six roots equally onto this object—this is six-roots-integrated recollection.

**Layer Two: Maintaining the link.** After the object is established, the next step is maintenance.

Maintenance is not grasping tightly. Grasping tightly is a Layer Two misreading—interpreting "maintenance" as "effortful concentration" is the most common recollection error. It is not an error; it is a misunderstanding. The result of this misunderstanding is fatigue, irritation, and recollection becoming a task.

The correct understanding of maintenance is: the object naturally exists in the mind; you do not need to effortfully keep it there; you only need to not intentionally look away. Like placing a lamp in a room—once lit, you do not need to keep pushing the switch; the lamp stays lit. Recollection's maintenance is the same: the object is lit in the mind; you only need not to intentionally look away.

The sign of maintenance is not "the mind is completely undisturbed"—that is the standard for dhyāna, not for recollection. The sign of recollection is: you know the object is still in your mind, even if it has faded, even if other thoughts have arisen—the object of recollection remains.

**Layer Three: The arising of confidence/joy.** When the object is maintained for a period, an effect automatically arises: confidence or joy.

This is not "religious emotion." It is not moving to tears, not full-body tingling, not a special experience. It is usually very subtle—you may not even notice it. It is merely a slight sensation of loosening, warmth, or stability in the mind.

The arising of confidence is recollection's internal reward—it is not an external reward; it is the necessary product of the operation. Like staring steadily at a lamp—the lamp does not get brighter, but you see light flowing in your eyes. Confidence is that light.

The importance of this step: it is the momentum that sustains recollection. Without confidence, recollection is a task; with confidence, recollection is practice. Confidence is not "you believe the Buddha will receive you"—that is faith. Recollection's confidence is "you see the internal operation of recollection actually working."

**Layer Four: Loosening attachment.** This is recollection's most core internal effect, the integration of the previous three layers.

Loosening attachment is not "abandoning attachment." You do not need to do anything to abandon attachment—attachment loosens automatically as long as you sustain the previous three layers.

Recollection's internal mechanism: when the object is stably maintained, the mind temporarily releases its daily attachment patterns (to self, to external objects, to emotion) and turns toward the object. This turn is not forced—it is natural. When the mind naturally turns toward the object, its intensity toward other attachment objects automatically decreases. This is not "you letting go"; it is "the mind loosening automatically."

The degree of loosening varies by practitioner. A beginner may loosen for only a few seconds—after those seconds, attachment returns. This is not failure. Those few seconds are enough to change the mind's structure—even if only for a few microseconds. Repeated brief loosening accumulates into structural change.

An advanced practitioner may sustain loosening for longer periods in one session. But even advanced practitioners cannot maintain permanent loosening. Loosening is a process, not a result. The purpose of recollection is not to achieve permanent loosening, but to repeat this loosening process in every recollection.

The method of testing whether loosening has occurred is Jiaoguang's verb classification: during recollection, are you using distinguishing-awareness recollection or six-roots-integrated recollection? The former means you are still using the sixth consciousness to analytically dissect the Buddha's qualities—a structure of "I am recollecting, the Buddha is the object," with manas's attachment still operating. The latter means six roots are equal and pacified—the structure of attachment is loosening automatically while you recollect. Both types can occur simultaneously in recollection, but in different proportions. A beginner's recollection is mostly distinguishing-awareness with occasional moments of six-roots-integration—those moments are the loosening.
